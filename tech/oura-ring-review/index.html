1. Introduction — Why a Ring?

I've worn a lot of wearables. A chest strap that made me look like I was prepping for surgery. A chunky fitness watch that left a tan line and drained its battery before Thursday. A clip-on sleep tracker I lost under my bed after two weeks.

So when smart rings started showing up everywhere — on the fingers of biohackers, athletes, and people who just wanted to know why they felt exhausted every Monday — I was curious but not convinced.

Then I tried the Oura Ring Gen 3.

I've been wearing it daily for six weeks now, sleeping with it, showering with it, and honestly forgetting it's there most of the time. That last part is the point. In a market crowded with devices that demand your attention, the Oura Ring does the opposite — it sits quietly on your finger, collects an enormous amount of health data, and waits for you to ask the right questions.

But here's what I want to be upfront about before we get into it: this ring costs money upfront, and then requires a $5.99 a month subscription to access most of the useful features. That's a real number. Whether it's a fair number is something we'll get into by the end of this review.

2. Design & Build Quality — Wearing It Every Day

The first thing people ask when they see the Oura Ring is some version of "wait, that's a fitness tracker?"

It doesn't look like one. It looks like a piece of jewellery you'd pick up at a boutique in Copenhagen. The ring is machined from titanium, finished with a DLC (diamond-like carbon) coating, and available in five colourways: Silver, Black, Gold, Stealth, and Rose Gold. I've been wearing the Stealth finish — a matte black — and after six weeks of daily wear, it looks almost exactly as it did on day one.

There's no screen. No buttons. No charging port. Just a smooth, clean band with a slightly raised inner housing that holds all the sensors.

3. Sleep Tracking Accuracy — The Core Promise

Sleep tracking is what Oura built its name on. Before the fitness industry fully woke up to the idea that recovery matters as much as training, Oura was already in the weeds — measuring not just how long you slept, but how well.

How the Ring Tracks Your Sleep

The ring uses a combination of photoplethysmography (PPG) — the same optical technology in most fitness trackers — plus an infrared temperature sensor and a 3-axis accelerometer. Together, these measure your heart rate, blood oxygen levels, skin temperature, and physical movement throughout the night.

From that raw data, the app reconstructs a picture of your sleep architecture: how long you spent in Light sleep, Deep sleep (slow-wave), and REM sleep.

The Readiness Score: The Number That Changes Your Day

This is where the Oura Ring gets genuinely useful — and honestly a little eerie.

Every morning, the app assigns you a Readiness Score. It's not just about last night's sleep. It factors in your HRV trend, resting heart rate, skin temperature deviation, activity load from the previous days, and sleep consistency over time.

"A score in the 85–100 range says: you're recovered, push hard today. A score in the 60s says: pull back. Below 60, the app is essentially asking whether you feel okay."

I've found this score to be unnervingly predictive. On days where I ignored a low Readiness Score and hammered a hard workout anyway, I paid for it the next day.

4. Health & Activity Tracking — Beyond the Bedtime

HRV: The Most Useful Number Most People Ignore

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) is the variation in time between your heartbeats. A higher, more variable HRV generally signals that your autonomic nervous system is in good shape. A dropping trend suggests stress, illness, overtraining, or poor sleep is catching up with you.

Oura measures HRV continuously during sleep and surfaces your nightly average, then tracks your personal trend over 30 days. This is important: your HRV isn't meaningfully compared to a stranger's. It's compared to your own baseline.

Skin Temperature & Illness Detection

I got sick in week four of testing. Two days before I felt any symptoms, my skin temperature trend had already crept 1.4°C above my baseline and my Readiness Score had dropped into the low 60s. That's not magic — it's consistent measurement. But it's highly useful.

5. The App Experience — Where the Data Comes to Life

Opening the Oura app for the first time is satisfying. The home screen greets you with your three core scores — Readiness, Sleep, and Activity — displayed as clean circular rings. It's immediately legible.

Tap into any score and you get a breakdown: which contributing factors helped, which dragged it down, and a brief plain-English explanation of what it means. "Your HRV was lower than usual last night — consider keeping today's workouts moderate." It's not revolutionary advice, but it's contextualised, and that context is what makes it stick.

6. Battery Life & Charging — The Real-World Numbers

Oura claims 4 to 7 days of battery life. With all features enabled — continuous heart rate, SpO2 monitoring, and skin temperature tracking — I consistently landed at 5 to 6 days between charges.

The ring charges on a small magnetic dock. A full charge from empty takes roughly 60 to 80 minutes. The one real inconvenience: you will need to take the ring off to charge, which means picking your window carefully. I charge mine during my morning shower. The goal is to never charge it at night — because a night without the ring is a night without sleep data.

7. The Subscription Model — The Real Cost

Let's be direct: the Oura Ring is not just a hardware purchase. It's a purchase plus an ongoing membership fee.

Oura Membership costs $5.99 per month — or roughly $72 a year. If you buy the ring and decline the subscription, you still get your three core daily scores, but you lose access to: detailed sleep stage analysis, cycle insights, resilience tracking, and advanced trend graphs.

Is the subscription worth it? For most people who buy this ring, yes. The features locked behind membership are exactly the features you bought the ring to access. Without them, you're using a Ferrari to go grocery shopping.

8. Pros & Cons — The Honest Summary

The High Notes

  • Best-in-class sleep tracking: Deep, nuanced data that goes beyond basic "time in bed."
  • Actionable HRV trending: Actually changes your behaviour and workout intensity.
  • Unobtrusive design: Looks like elegant jewellery, not a clinical fitness tracker.
  • Illness detection: Accurately flags temperature changes before symptoms hit.

The Trade-offs

  • The Subscription: $5.99/month is a real, unavoidable cost to access the best features.
  • No GPS: Cannot replace a Garmin or Apple Watch for serious runners or cyclists.
  • Sealed Battery: Degrades over a few years and cannot be replaced.

9. Alternatives & Competitors

Samsung Galaxy Ring: The most obvious competitor. It offers a similar titanium build and health tracking, but with no subscription fee. The trade-off is that Oura's software and sleep algorithms are currently much more mature. Also, Samsung's ring is best for Android/Galaxy users.

WHOOP 4.0: Aimed at serious athletes. No upfront cost, but a hefty $30/month subscription. It tracks cardiovascular strain much better than Oura, but it's a visible wristband, not a discrete ring.

10. The Final Verdict

After six weeks on my finger, the Oura Ring Gen 3 has done something no other wearable managed: it made me care about recovery as much as performance. The shift in perspective, from output-focused to recovery-aware, is the ring's most valuable contribution.

If sleep and recovery are your primary health goals, the Oura Ring is still the smartest smart ring for health tracking you can buy. Just go in with your eyes open regarding the subscription fee.

FAQ

Is the Oura Ring worth the subscription?

For most users, yes. The $5.99/month subscription unlocks the features (trend analysis, cycle insights, HRV context) that make the ring genuinely useful rather than just informative. But if you only want basic daily scores, you can skip it.

How accurate is Oura Ring sleep tracking?

Oura's sleep tracking is among the best in consumer wearables. Total sleep time and wakefulness detection are highly accurate. Sleep stage classification (Light vs. REM) is slightly less reliable than clinical equipment but highly useful for personal trend analysis.

Oura Ring vs. Samsung Galaxy Ring — which is better?

Oura has more mature software, deeper sleep insights, and works on both iOS and Android. Samsung has no subscription fee and better ecosystem integration for Galaxy users. If budget is a concern, Samsung wins on total cost of ownership.
